How to Actually Self-Study

A complete method that works for any stream and any class — the route no coaching institute teaches you.

Phase 1 — First Read

Before your teacher even opens the chapter

1
📖 Read NCERT without picking up a pen
Open the chapter. Read it from start to finish — without making any notes, without highlighting, without a pen in hand.

If you don't understand something, do not stop. Keep reading. Your only goal here is to get a feel for the chapter. Understanding comes later. This first pass plants the chapter in your brain so everything after this sticks better.
2
📅 Read it before your teacher teaches it
Before your school teacher covers a chapter, you should have read it at least once on your own. When the teacher then explains it in class, it will feel like revision — not new information. Concepts will click faster, and your class time becomes twice as valuable.
Phase 2 — In the Classroom

What to do when the teacher is teaching

3
👂 Listen fully. Make class notes IN class.
When your teacher is teaching, your only job is to listen and make notes right there — not at home later by watching a YouTube video.
  • Make notes directly from what the teacher says — in your own words
  • Ask doubts in class — not after going home and "studying" the doubt away
  • Don't go home and copy class notes from YouTube — that is not studying, that is procrastinating with a pen
Phase 3 — After Class (Same Day)

The rough notebook method — before memory fades

4
📝 Write the topic in your Rough Notebook — from memory
After the class ends, open your Rough Notebook and write down everything you remember about the topic — without looking at any book or notes. This is the single most powerful thing you can do for memory.
If you can recall it → Great. The topic is entering your long-term memory.
If you can't recall it → Re-read that specific topic in NCERT the same day. Try to understand it yourself first.
Still not clear → Go to YouTube, find a video on that specific topic, watch it, then solve any NCERT questions on that topic to confirm understanding.
Phase 4 — Deep NCERT Reading

After all topics in the chapter are done

5
✍️ Read the full NCERT chapter carefully — and highlight
Once all topics in a chapter are covered in class, go back and read the entire NCERT chapter once more — this time slowly and carefully. Highlight the most important lines and concepts.

By your second read, you are not reading to understand — you are reading to remember. Highlighting forces you to actively decide what matters.
6
🧪 Solve NCERT + NCERT Examples, then PYQ
After finishing the chapter twice:
  1. Solve all NCERT in-text questions and examples.
  2. Then solve Previous Year Questions (PYQ) from your own — no AI, no shortcuts.
  3. Mark every question you couldn't solve the first time. These become your revision targets.
7
📐 Make a Formula Sheet
Wherever formulas, key dates, definitions, or rules matter — maintain a dedicated Formula Sheet (a separate small notebook or sheets). Keep adding to it. This becomes your fastest revision tool before exams.
The Star Rating System

Rate every question as you solve it

While solving NCERT, examples, and PYQ — mark each question with a star rating. This makes your revision dramatically faster.

★ 1 Star — Easy question
Direct formula application. You solved it easily without thinking. During exam, if you are short on time, you can safely attempt these last — you already know them. They take the least effort to revise.
★★ 2 Stars — Couldn't solve it even twice
You attempted this question and couldn't crack it even on a second try. During revision, give these 2nd priority. But — and this is critical:
You must find out why you couldn't solve it. Is there a concept you're missing? A formula you don't know? Find the root and fix it — don't just re-read the solution and move on.
★★★ 3 Stars — Most important / most time-consuming
Questions that are frequently asked in exams, take the most time, or cover the most marks. These get 1st priority during revision. Master these and you have covered most of what the exam will test.
Exam Revision Strategy

How to revise when exam time comes

Revise in this exact order — Reverse Manner
★★★ → ★★ → ★
Hardest and most important first. Easy questions last.
  • ⭐ Revise all ★★★ questions — your highest priority material
  • ⭐ Revise all ★★ questions — fix the concepts you were weak on
  • ⭐ Re-read the highlighted lines of your NCERT
  • ⭐ Revise your Formula Sheet
  • ⭐ Skim ★ questions — just a quick check that you still know them

By the time exams arrive, you should have read the NCERT at least 3 times — the first read (before teaching), the deep read (highlighted), and the revision read. This is not extreme. This is what students who score well actually do.

Your Notebook Setup

What notebooks to keep and what goes in each

📝 Class Notebook
Notes made in class while the teacher teaches. Keep it clean and organized — you'll revise from this.
🖊️ Rough Notebook
Where you write topics from memory after class. Also for working out problems, rough calculations, and first-attempt thinking.
📐 Formula Sheet
Dedicated notebook or sheets for formulas, key dates, important definitions, and rules. Your fastest exam revision tool.
📖 PYQ Notebook
Solve all Previous Year Questions here. Mark star ratings on each question. Keep this as a record of your progress.
🧮 How to make notes (write less, show more)
Instead of writing long paragraphs, convert information into flow charts, diagrams, and tables. A single well-drawn table covers what would take a full page of writing — and it is far easier to revise from.
Critical Rules

Things that can quietly derail your preparation

⚠️ Never mix School Exams with Competitive Exam prep
School exams and entrance exams (JEE, NEET, CUET, CLAT, etc.) are completely different in style, depth, and approach. Treat them as separate tracks. When studying for school boards, focus on boards. When preparing for competitive exams, shift modes. Mixing them confuses both.
⚠️ No AI for solving questions
When solving PYQ and NCERT questions, do it yourself — completely. The struggle of not being able to solve something is exactly where learning happens. The moment you look up the answer, you rob yourself of that growth. If you are stuck for 20 minutes, look at a hint — not a solution.
💡 This method can save you money too
This route — reading NCERT 3 times, rough notebook recall, star-rating questions, revising in reverse — is what students who score well actually do. Most coaching institutes will never tell you this because it does not require them. It requires you. But it works, and it is free.
